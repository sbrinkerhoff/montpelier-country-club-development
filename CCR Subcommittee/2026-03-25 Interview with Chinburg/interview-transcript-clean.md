### Project Overview Presentation (Paul Goodwin)

**Paul Goodwin:** Our proposal focuses on a village center type development strategy. We look to focus development efforts on the lower portion of the site to better utilize the city's investment in infrastructure and deliver more housing more quickly in a compact form. It's also more efficient in terms of keeping the maximum area open for recreation or conservation and encouraging alternative transportation like walking or biking.

We have roughly 310 units proposed in two phases: 250 apartments and 60 for-sale homes. The first phase focuses on the lowermost buildable portion of the site to maximize efficiency for housing delivery and public infrastructure investments.

I do want to step back and say that when putting together our RFP response, we did not fully appreciate the critical nature of the existing recreational facilities. We thought it was more of a recommendation. The team is very open to alternative site plans, reworking this plan to accommodate existing recreational facilities and better align with the community's needs. This was our first pass.

Key elements of the proposal:
- Retaining the existing child care facility with an adjacent play space and a sense of arrival with a landing park or quad
- A central public green utilizing the site's topography for stormwater management
- Transitioning from denser multifamily apartment structures creating the backbone of the site, down to townhomes and then single-family homes — a traditional New England village structure
- Up to 10,000 square feet of optional supporting retail uses focused in the multifamily buildings along the green
- The northern area of the site, where topography is less welcoming for development, is an excellent opportunity for conservation or recreation. A potential future third phase, but not currently anticipated
- Parking planned as surface parking (off-street and on-street) rather than structured parking, though we're open to reassessing as the project progresses

**Eric Chinburg:** I'm a fan of trying to get structured parking to work in this pro forma. We've done a little looking at the topography and think it's viable. There's a path forward to increasing green space by adding parking under the larger buildings — not a parking garage, just on the lower level of the larger multiunit buildings.

**Sal Alfano:** So you're thinking underground?

**Eric Chinburg:** Correct. No additional structures, and there would be more green space.

**Paul Goodwin:** A good number of our ground-up developments utilize either subsurface parking under the building or some portion of the first story, usually the rear.

### Q&A: Building Scale

**Sal Alfano:** There's been concern about the size of the phase one apartment building — 144 units. It's a pretty immense building. I don't think there's anything close to it anywhere near Washington County. Would you talk about why that is?

**Paul Goodwin:** We are by no means committed to that building footprint. It's just emblematic of what could be. A four-story building is not out of scale with the community, though the length is perhaps unprecedented for Montpelier. That's easily remedied — it could be reduced to a number of smaller buildings to either maintain the unit count or reduce density. Obviously, there are limitations to project viability when you reduce overall unit count.

**Eric Chinburg:** I could definitely see taking the larger building and breaking it into two. One of the goals is to create reasonably affordable housing. It's much easier with one building, one elevator stack, and two sets of egress stairs serving a larger building. If we chop buildings up much more than cutting the larger one in half, you really start to struggle with cost per unit and affordability becomes more difficult. It's a trade-off.

**Paul Goodwin:** We're showing three different multifamily building typologies. The larger building is more efficient — less facade materials, less internal circulation per unit, easier to include resident amenities like a club room or fitness center. The smaller buildings are about 12 apartments each, typically without elevators. This is purely our first pass. We understand this is an iterative process.

### Q&A: Unit Mix and Market Assumptions

**Ben Doyle:** I'm wondering about flexibility in the types of units. The total mix is 250 apartment units — 40% studios, 40% one-bedrooms. What are the market assumptions you're basing that on, and is there flexibility to have more multi-bedroom units?

**Eric Chinburg:** We're open to what the community desires. We've done a lot of work in New Hampshire and Maine trying to create reasonably affordable apartments. The smaller the apartment, the easier it is to afford. A lot of the need is from recent college graduates, divorcees, or empty nesters. Three-bedroom apartments are an economic challenge — the apartment gets big and you can't keep charging the same per square foot. But we could add some three-bedrooms.

**Paul Goodwin:** This unit mix comes from our experience managing 2,000+ units. For a rental product, it's typically not more than two bedrooms. Rental properties are typically not child-dense — very few households with school-aged children move into rental properties in our region. The demand for more bedrooms and households with children is likely higher pressure on the for-sale units, which are mostly targeted to be three bedrooms.

### Q&A: Modular Construction

**Sal Alfano:** Have you considered or worked with modular construction?

**Eric Chinburg:** We've done panelized construction in the past. It generally didn't save money but saved a little time. We recently compared full modular versus stick-built for the Charlestown houses, and stick-built was still less expensive. We have really fresh data on that.

**Paul Goodwin:** I've done at-scale modular building and can reaffirm what Eric said. It is not more cost-effective. It is more time-efficient with a well-coordinated team, and more efficient from a waste stream perspective since it's built in a controlled environment. But it's not a tool in the toolbox to make the project more viable.

### Q&A: Site Visit and Project Risks

**Adrian Gill:** Have you all visited the site?

**Paul Goodwin:** I have not.

**Eric Chinburg:** I have not either.

**Adrian Gill:** I live in Montpelier. I think it's one of the most beautiful pieces of land in Montpelier. It's heavily used for recreation — soccer, families up there seven days a week, the day care center with kids doing outdoor play. What are the risks from your perspective?

**Eric Chinburg:** I've long known about four areas of risk in these projects:
1. **Entitlement risk** — we have no idea if we're going to be doing the project or if what we want to do will get approved by the planning department.
2. **Construction cost risk** — I feel very good about because we control the construction company.
3. **Interest rate risk** — rental properties are very dependent on reasonable interest rates. We have great relationships with lenders and often do forward swaps to fix the current rate into the future.
4. **Market risk** — the biggest one. If we build it, will they come? If we do a $75 million project and start opening doors and there isn't a market, that can keep you up at night.

I have no fear of execution risk. We can build this and work with the community.

### Q&A: Infrastructure Financing (CHIP and Catalyst)

**Brian Pine:** It would be helpful to hear how you envision the infrastructure — not just phasing, but who's paying for construction of infrastructure both to the site and on the site, and who retains ownership and responsibility going forward.

**Ben Doyle:** To put a finer point on it — the CHIP financing contemplated for this site can be backstopped by either the municipality or the developer. To what degree would you be willing to consider that as a partnership, or do you see this strictly as the city's responsibility?

**Paul Goodwin:** We understand the CHIP program is a tool that becomes part of the capital stack for making the project work. We're hoping that CHIP combined with the Catalyst grant will provide the backbone enabling infrastructure — rebuilding Country Club Road and building out the core proposed street network. Secondary access like alleys and circulation within lots would be funded and maintained privately. We understand the CHIP program requires our participation and this is a partnership. We're open to taking on some of those responsibilities as we refine the proposal. It's hard to put a fine number on infrastructure needs until we have a site plan the city is greenlighting.

**Eric Chinburg:** On the schematic, the green roads would be done by the municipality and purple would be us?

**Paul Goodwin:** Yes. Country Club Road improvements would likely be covered by the Catalyst grant. The green roadways are CHIP-eligible areas. Purple would be purely privately funded.

**Brian Pine:** Who carries the burden for securing financing? Is that something you're willing to entertain if the city says we're not in a position to bond for this? There are two options under CHIP: the municipality goes to the bond bank, or you as the private entity acquire private financing. The CHIP approach is intended to bypass the lengthy voter approval process that only happens once a year in March.

**Ben Doyle:** You shouldn't feel bad — no one's done one. It's a new program. You could be the first.

**Mike Miller:** The payment mechanism is the same — tax revenue pays the bond. In a typical TIF, the city takes out the bond and collects tax revenue to pay it. CHIP allows you to take out the bond or loan. We get the tax revenue and use it to pay your bond. It just puts the risk on you. If you fail to achieve your increment, that falls more on you than on city taxpayers.

**Eric Chinburg:** Assuming we would be part of creating the increment and it's a reasonable test, I wouldn't have any problem with us borrowing that money if the city is basically paying the debt service. If we thought it was a reasonable risk, that's certainly something we could talk about.

### Q&A: Brownfield Remediation

**Ben Doyle:** You've done a lot of urban and mill redevelopment. There's been a phase one and phase two done on this site, but there's still additional testing to be done and no corrective action plan in place. Can you speak to your experience with brownfield remediation?

**Matt Asia:** We had our environmental consultant look at the phase one. It's a former golf course — pesticides were used. The first two feet of material have those pesticides, including the topsoil. Two options: reuse the material on-site through a soil management plan — creating berms and landscaping features — or worst case, go off-site to a landfill. We typically work very hard not to send things to landfills. We have experience working with brownfield consultants and lenders, and there are programs that will fund some of this work. We're not scared of that.

**Eric Chinburg:** We've probably done projects on a dozen brownfield sites.

**Sal Alfano:** Your current cost plans don't take remediation into account — it's based on a clean field?

**Matt Asia:** Cost-wise, yes — only in the sense that we didn't plan to move material to a landfill. Moving material on-site is baked into the general conceptual numbers.

**Paul Goodwin:** At this level of analysis, it's beyond the RFP scope, but it's certainly something that would be refined, and we have deep experience doing that.

### Q&A: Market Risk and Financial Resilience

**Sal Alfano:** How comfortable are you with your marketing analysis for the quantity and type of units proposed? What happens if it doesn't pan out after phase one?

**Eric Chinburg:** Our lenders underwrite to a debt coverage ratio of at least 1.2. If we're off by 20%, we have a breakeven project — meaning we don't pay any return to investors, but we still have the money to pay the bank. The project will need about $20 million in equity. We'll likely raise that from a close group of investors who understand us and have been loyal. Worst case, investors won't get a return on their investment.

Our approach to market analysis: we look at Montpelier's relationship to Burlington — they're forty minutes apart. Burlington rents are higher. We project rents commensurate with the Montpelier market. We've done this in Saco, Maine and Lewiston, Maine — comparing to Portland rents and applying discounts based on distance and market conditions. Then we push it forward three years and assume rents go up 2% a year. We plug those numbers in and see if it works.

**Paul Goodwin:** It's very difficult in a place like Montpelier to have a traditional market study. In Boston, there are dozens of new housing projects that tell you about rents and absorption. The data points are just thinner in places where we work — Lewiston, Maine, Claremont, New Hampshire, Franklin, New Hampshire. What we rely on is that these communities have a lot of pent-up demand for new housing. Many haven't had meaningful new development in a long time. Giving people an option for low-maintenance, energy-efficient, ADA-accessible housing — these things don't exist in the existing housing stock. That pent-up demand is hard to quantify but has made a lot of our projects very successful.

**Brian Pine:** Banks require market studies, but they're cookie-cutter studies that aren't that useful for what we're talking about here. We're working with a group that does much more in-depth market studies. I'll talk to Paul, Matt, and Eric offline about that approach.

### Public Comment — Steve Whitaker

**Sal Alfano:** We have about ten minutes left. I'm going to open it up for public comment. Mr. Whitaker?

**Steve Whitaker:** My name is Steve Whitaker. I'm concerned that this process has skipped the planning that was supposed to come first. Vermont statute assigns master planning responsibility to the planning commission with mandatory public participation. White and Burke's own 2023 deliverable was explicit in describing a phase two master planning process. RFP development was to follow environmental assessment, community working groups, and housing needs assessment. None of those prerequisites were completed. The planning commission was sidelined. The working groups were never convened. Twenty-seven months after the flood, the city jumped straight to developer selection.

The public statutory right to shape this decision was bypassed. Under the city charter, approximately 300 registered voter signatures can force a legally binding article onto the city meeting warning requiring voter approval before any land disposition. That option remains on the table.

The contract that launched this process was kept secret. The White and Burke agreement was never disclosed before the council voted to approve it at midnight. A public records request filed before that vote was refused. The contract contains a 3% contingent fee tied to grand list value increases from whatever you build here, paid out over years post-closing, giving the consultants steering the selection a direct financial stake in the intensity of your development. That structure was never disclosed to the public. It raises conflict concerns under federal procurement standards attached to the funding streams this project depends on.

That contract is now the subject of a preliminary injunction proceeding in superior court. A related matter is before the Vermont Supreme Court challenging regional approval of the city plan, which omitted a CCR master plan. The legal foundation of the process you're interviewed under today is genuinely contested.

You can't propose a site plan here with the ground truth missing. The property is an umbrella-enrolled brownfield with documented contamination of lead, arsenic, mercury, and heptachlor epoxide. The phase two ESA is incomplete. The supplemental site assessment — which defines buildable versus restricted areas and determines cleanup costs — has no approved work plan and has not even commenced. You don't have any real idea where you can build. You don't know what remediation will cost, and you can't responsibly propose TIF or CHIP financing when the land may carry a stigma of toxicity that suppresses rather than increases taxable value.

The civic framework hasn't been established either. This isn't just a housing site. Light industrial, commerce, community facilities, disaster resilience, recreation, and transportation requirements haven't been programmed. Traffic capacity on Route 2 at CCR has never been analyzed for the scale contemplated. The city's own officials have told regional planning bodies there's no housing project while simultaneously representing to federal funders that the infrastructure will support hundreds of units. That inconsistency is now the subject of federal compliance challenges filed with HUD and the Department of Transportation.

What you're being asked to propose is a site plan without environmental clearance, a finance structure without a known tax base, a housing program without a civic framework, and a transaction whose legal authorization is under active judicial challenge. I'm not here to stop housing in Montpelier. I'm here to promote honest governance and accountability, because a developer who proceeds under these conditions is not building on solid ground.
