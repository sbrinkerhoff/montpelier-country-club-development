## Summary

This was the first of four developer interviews for the city-owned Country Club Road (CCR) site. Chinburg Properties presented a village-center concept proposing approximately 310 housing units (250 apartments, 60 for-sale homes) in two phases, focused on the lower portion of the site. The interview covered site design, building scale, unit mix, construction approach, energy efficiency, infrastructure financing, brownfield remediation, market risk, recreation preservation, and traffic access. The Chinburg team repeatedly emphasized flexibility and willingness to iterate on the plan in partnership with the city. Public comment raised significant procedural, legal, and environmental concerns about the overall selection process.

## Key Discussion Topics

### 1. Project Overview
Paul Goodwin presented the proposal: a village-center development on the lower portion of the CCR site with approximately 310 units in two phases — 250 apartments and 60 for-sale homes. The design transitions from denser multifamily buildings to townhomes to single-family homes in a New England village pattern. Up to 10,000 sq ft of optional retail was included for placemaking. The northern portion of the site was reserved for conservation/recreation with a possible future third phase.

The team acknowledged they did not fully appreciate the critical importance of the existing recreational facilities when preparing their RFP response and stated they are very open to reworking the site plan to accommodate them.

### 2. Building Scale and the 144-Unit Building
The proposed phase one includes a large 144-unit, four-story apartment building — unprecedented in scale for Washington County. Chinburg indicated they are not committed to that footprint; it was a first-pass efficiency play. Eric Chinburg said he could see splitting it into two buildings. Paul Goodwin noted three different multifamily typologies were shown (large elevator building, mid-size, and 12-unit walk-ups). Reducing building count affects cost-per-unit and affordability due to loss of economies of scale (single elevator stack, shared egress, centralized amenities).

### 3. Unit Mix and Affordability
The proposed apartment mix is 40% studios, 40% one-bedrooms, and 20% two-bedrooms. Ben Doyle asked about flexibility for more multi-bedroom units. Eric Chinburg explained the mix is driven by market demand (college grads, divorcees, empty nesters) and the economics of three-bedroom apartments being difficult to pencil out. Paul Goodwin added that rental properties in their region are typically not child-dense, and demand for family-sized units is better served by the for-sale homes (targeted at three bedrooms).

### 4. Homeownership vs. Rentals and School Enrollment
Adrian Gill raised the community's desire for more homeownership to attract families and increase school enrollment, noting Montpelier already has a disproportionate amount of subsidized rental housing. She asked about smaller, more affordable for-sale homes. Eric Chinburg was enthusiastic, citing a pilot project in Charlestown, NH building 1,400 sq ft homes at approximately $500,000 (breakeven). He suggested the smaller 12-unit apartment buildings could be converted to two-bedroom townhouses. The team expressed strong willingness to shift the rental/ownership balance.

### 5. Modular and Panelized Construction
Sal Alfano asked about modular construction. Both Eric Chinburg and Paul Goodwin reported that based on recent direct experience, modular is not more cost-effective than stick-built — it can save time and reduce waste but does not reduce cost. Chinburg has fresh comparative data from the Charlestown project.

### 6. Construction Costs and Vertical Integration
Ben Doyle noted Chinburg's per-square-foot costs were considerably lower than other proposals and asked how they achieve that, especially with limited Vermont experience. Eric Chinburg explained they are vertically integrated — owning both the development and construction companies (building approximately 100 houses and 400 apartments per year). This eliminates adversarial change-order dynamics. Their construction president, Rob Nunez, has extensive regional experience, and they have a track record of building strong local subcontractor relationships in new markets (citing Lewiston, ME and Bennington, VT).


### 7. Energy Efficiency and HVAC
Paul Goodwin confirmed all buildings would target Energy Star compliance at minimum, with all-electric heat pumps for heating and cooling (Vermont code essentially requires all-electric). Eric Chinburg noted 100% of homes built in the last 15 years are Energy Star certified, and they have received national awards for Energy Star construction seven or eight years running.

### 8. Structured Parking
The initial proposal relied on surface parking to avoid the cost of structured parking. Eric Chinburg advocated for exploring subsurface parking under the larger buildings (not a standalone garage) to increase green space. The team has extensive experience with first-floor and basement-level parking in ground-up developments.

### 9. Recreation Preservation
Sal Alfano emphasized that the flattest, most heavily used soccer fields sit directly in the proposed building areas and represent some of the last flat, non-flooding land in Montpelier. Adrian Gill highlighted the need to coordinate with Turtle Island (the outdoor day care center on-site). Paul Goodwin acknowledged the misalignment, took responsibility for underestimating the recreation commitment, and expressed confidence that their design team (which includes new urbanist consultants) could rework the plan to preserve existing recreation facilities. Eric Chinburg suggested eliminating the smaller apartment buildings to maintain the fields while focusing density in a reconfigured version of the large building.

### 10. Infrastructure Financing (CHIP and Catalyst)
Brian Pine and Ben Doyle asked about the developer's expectations for infrastructure financing. Paul Goodwin presented a diagram showing: Catalyst grant funding for Country Club Road improvements, CHIP-eligible funding for the core internal street network (green roads), and privately funded secondary access/alleys (purple roads). The team was unfamiliar with the CHIP program mechanics but expressed willingness to take on the borrowing if the city's tax increment covers debt service. Mike Miller clarified that under CHIP, the developer can secure the financing (vs. municipal bonding), but the risk shifts to the developer if the tax increment falls short. Eric Chinburg said he would be comfortable with that risk if they participate in determining the increment and deem it reasonable. Infrastructure and housing construction would need to proceed largely in lockstep, with infrastructure slightly ahead.

### 11. Brownfield Remediation
Ben Doyle asked about brownfield experience. Matt Asia reported their environmental consultant reviewed the phase one assessment — the former golf course has pesticide contamination in the top two feet of soil. Preferred approach: on-site reuse via soil management plan (berms, landscaping features). Worst case: off-site landfill disposal, which they work hard to avoid. Eric Chinburg noted they have done projects on approximately a dozen brownfield sites. Current cost estimates do not include landfill disposal but do account for on-site material movement.

### 12. Market Risk and Financial Resilience
Eric Chinburg described their market analysis approach: benchmarking Montpelier rents against Burlington (discounted approximately 20% for distance/market), projecting forward three years at 2% annual growth. Lenders underwrite to a 1.2x debt coverage ratio, meaning a 20% miss results in breakeven (no investor returns but bank debt is covered). The project would require approximately $20M in equity from their established investor group. Paul Goodwin added that smaller New England markets have thin data compared to Boston, but they have found significant pent-up demand for new, energy-efficient, ADA-accessible housing in communities that have not seen meaningful new development in years. Brian Pine noted he would connect the Chinburg team with a firm doing more in-depth market studies than the standard bank-required approach.

### 13. Traffic and Site Access
Sal Alfano asked about the team's position that a single access point is sufficient. Paul Goodwin said this is based on past experience with projects of similar magnitude and noted that a big-box store generates more traffic than this development would. He acknowledged it needs validation and said they are open to multiple access points if the community desires them for connectivity or circulation reasons.

### 14. Site Visit
Neither Paul Goodwin nor Eric Chinburg have visited the site. Adrian Gill described it as one of the most beautiful properties in Montpelier, heavily used for recreation seven days a week.

## Public Comment — Steve Whitaker

Steve Whitaker raised a series of procedural, legal, and environmental objections to the overall CCR development process (not specific to Chinburg's proposal):

- Vermont statute assigns master planning to the planning commission with mandatory public participation; the planning commission was sidelined and community working groups were never convened.
- The White and Burke consulting contract was not disclosed before the council vote; it contains a 3% contingent fee tied to grand list value increases, creating a potential conflict of interest.
- That contract is the subject of a preliminary injunction in superior court; a related matter challenging regional approval of the city plan is before the Vermont Supreme Court.
- The property is an umbrella-enrolled brownfield with documented contamination (lead, arsenic, mercury, heptachlor epoxide); the phase two ESA is incomplete and the supplemental site assessment has not commenced.
- Traffic capacity on Route 2 at CCR has never been analyzed for the contemplated scale.
- Federal compliance challenges have been filed with HUD and the Department of Transportation regarding CDBG-DR funding requirements.
- Under the city charter, approximately 300 voter signatures can force a binding article requiring voter approval before any land disposition.

Whitaker stated he is not opposed to housing in Montpelier but is advocating for honest governance and accountability.
